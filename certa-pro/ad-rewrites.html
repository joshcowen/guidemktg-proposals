Ad Rewrites

Here's what we changed in your current ads and why. Every edit comes back to one shift: moving from positioning Certa Pro as the hero to positioning your customer as the hero — with Certa Pro as the guide who helps them win.

The Core Idea

Your customer is the hero. You're the guide.

Most companies market themselves the same way: lead with credentials, list services, and ask for the sale. And most customers tune it out, because nothing in that message is about them.

The StoryBrand framework flips this. Your customer is walking around with a problem they want solved. They're not looking for a company that's been around 14 years — they're looking for someone who understands their frustration and can make it go away. When your message leads with their pain first, they stop scrolling.

These rewrites don't change who Certa Pro is. They change who the story is about.

What changed and why

HEADLINE
From credential to pain point
"Grand Haven's Most Trusted Painters" is a claim about Certa Pro. The reader hears it and thinks "sure, everyone says that." The rewrite opens with the fear the customer is already carrying — is this person going to show up? That's the real question in every homeowner's head before they hire a painter. When your headline names that fear, they stop scrolling because you're talking about them, not about you. The character has a problem. Lead with their problem, not your resume.
BODY OPENING
From Certa Pro's story to their story
The original opens with "Certa Pro Painters has a 4.7-star rating for a reason." That sentence makes Certa Pro the subject. The rewrite opens with "Most Grand Haven homeowners have at least one contractor story they'd rather forget." Now the customer is the subject. You're showing empathy before you pitch anything — which is the guide move. The customer needs to feel understood before they'll listen to your solution. A guide demonstrates empathy before authority. Empathy first, credentials second.
SERVICES LIST
Cut "we do it all"
"Interior, exterior, cabinets, decks, we do it all" is pure hero language. It's a list of what Certa Pro offers, not what the customer gets. We replaced it with what the customer actually experiences: written estimate, daily updates, walkthrough at the end, two-year warranty. Same information, different frame — "here's your experience" instead of "here's our menu." The guide describes the plan — what the customer's experience will be — not just their capabilities.
SOCIAL PROOF
Moved to the end, reframed
The original leads with the star rating as proof Certa Pro is good. The rewrite saves social proof for the end and reframes it around the customer outcome: "4.7 stars from over 40 Grand Haven homeowners who finally got the experience they deserved." Same 4.7 stars — but now it's proof that other people like them solved the exact problem you just described. Authority comes after empathy. Proof closes the loop — it doesn't open the pitch.

What changed and why

HOOK
From questions to recognition
The original hook asks three questions — "Is your painter going to show up? Finish on time? Do it right?" That's not bad, but questions put the viewer in a passive position. The rewrite opens with a scene they recognize: "You've been here before." That phrase is a pattern interrupt. It tells the viewer you already know their story before they've said a word. Recognition is more powerful than a question. Open with the customer's internal problem, not just the external one. They're not worried about paint — they're worried about being burned again.
SCENE 1
Cut "we built the business around certainty"
The moment Scene 1 shifts to "at Certa Pro, we built the entire business around one word," the company becomes the hero. The rewrite keeps Scene 1 entirely about the viewer's experience — names their pain, validates their frustration, and ends with "your home deserves better than that." Certa Pro doesn't show up until Scene 2. That's the guide move: let the problem land fully before you offer the solution. Don't introduce yourself until you've shown you understand the problem. Jumping to your solution too fast feels like a pitch, not empathy.
SCENE 2
From "our system" to your experience
"Our Certa Pro Service System means you get..." makes the system the subject. The rewrite keeps you as the subject throughout: "you get a written estimate," "you're never left wondering," "you deserve to know it was done right." Same checkmarks, same features — but the customer is the one experiencing them, not Certa Pro delivering them. The text overlays also changed: "On time / On budget" (Certa Pro attributes) became "On time, every time / Daily updates" (customer experiences). Features are about you. Benefits framed around the customer's experience are about them. Always frame around them.
CTA
Kept the outcome, sharpened it
The original CTA was actually already close — "stop worrying and start loving your home again" is good customer-outcome language. We kept that spirit and made it a statement instead of a question: "Your home should feel like something you're proud of." Then added urgency without pressure: "it takes 2 minutes." Short, specific, removes the friction of a vague ask. The CTA should point toward the customer's success vision — what their life looks like after they work with you.
